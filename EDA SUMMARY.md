Low Spend Risk — Boxplots show churners have much lower total spend. Low‑value customers are less emotionally invested and easier to lose.

📝 >>> Explanation<<<
Customer churn emerges from a combination of behavioral, transactional, and engagement‑related signals, and the exploratory analysis highlights six strong hypotheses. First, loyalty tier is a powerful predictor. Customers in the Silver tier churn at a much higher rate than Gold or Platinum members, indicating that loyalty benefits, recognition, and accumulated rewards play a meaningful role in retention. Lower‑tier customers may not feel sufficiently valued or may not have built enough relationship depth with the brand.

Second, spending behavior shows a clear divide. Churners consistently fall into the low‑spend group, suggesting weaker product fit, lower satisfaction, or limited perceived value. High‑spend customers, by contrast, demonstrate stronger brand affinity and are less likely to churn. This aligns with the third hypothesis: order frequency. Customers with fewer orders or limited repeat purchases show higher churn, reinforcing the idea that habitual purchasing is a stabilizing force.

Support‑ticket patterns reveal another important dimension. Churners tend to have more support tickets, indicating friction, dissatisfaction, or unresolved issues. High ticket volume often signals pain points in product quality, delivery, or service experience. When these issues accumulate, customers disengage and eventually churn.

Engagement metrics from web and app behavior further strengthen the churn narrative. Churners exhibit fewer sessions, fewer product views, and lower interaction overall. This drop in engagement is often an early warning sign, reflecting declining interest or unmet expectations. Finally, the “last_visit_days_ago” metric shows that churners have been inactive for significantly longer periods. Inactivity is one of the strongest churn predictors across industries, as customers who stop visiting or browsing are unlikely to return.

Together, these six hypotheses form a coherent picture: churn is driven by low loyalty, low value, low engagement, and high friction. Each factor reinforces the others, creating a downward spiral that ultimately results in customer loss.
